## 中文版口播稿

标题建议：
你不是不会用 AI，你只是还没把它接进工作流

### 00:00 - 00:35 开场：真实焦虑

很多人以为，自己已经在用 AI 了。

每天打开 ChatGPT，问几个问题，让它改一段文案，总结一篇文章，写几个标题。

这些当然有用。

但我想说一句可能有点刺耳的话：

如果你只是这样用 AI，你和别人之间的差距，并不会被拉开。

真正的差距，不在于谁会写更花的 prompt。

而在于谁能把 AI 放进每天真实发生的工作流里。

因为接下来，大多数人的问题不是信息不够。

恰恰相反，是信息太多。

工具太多，新闻太多，观点太多，机会太多。

你每天看起来很忙，收藏了很多东西，转发了很多链接，脑子里塞满了“好像有用”的信息。

但到了晚上，你会发现，手上没有多推进一件真正重要的事。

这才是最危险的地方。

不是你没看到机会。

是你已经被信息流训练成了一个只会反应的人。

### 00:35 - 01:05 定义问题：别再只把 AI 当聊天框

所以今天我不讲某个新工具。

也不讲“AI 又要改变世界”这种大话。

我只讲一个所有知识工作者都应该先搭起来的基础 workflow：

AI 每日信息过滤器。

你可以把它理解成一个只为你工作的研究助理。

它每天不负责帮你刷更多新闻。

它只负责一件事：

从一堆杂乱信息里，筛出三条你今天真的值得行动的信号。

注意，是行动信号，不是新闻摘要。

这两者差别很大。

新闻摘要只会告诉你发生了什么。

行动信号会告诉你：这件事和你有什么关系，你下一步能做什么，以及要不要直接丢掉。

### 01:05 - 01:45 第一步：先定义你的过滤标准

这个 workflow 的第一步，不是打开 Notion，也不是接 API。

第一步是定义你的过滤标准。

很多人用 AI 整理信息，效果很差，是因为他上来就说：

“帮我总结这十篇文章。”

这句话的问题是，它只要求 AI 总结，并没有要求 AI 判断。

但你真正需要的是判断。

比如你是独立开发者，你可以这样定义标准：

第一，这个信息能不能在七天内变成一个小 demo？

第二，它背后有没有明确的用户痛点？

第三，它能不能接入我现有的工具链，比如 Notion、Telegram、Slack、Stripe、飞书？

第四，它有没有潜在付费场景？

第五，它适不适合一个人或者一个小团队执行？

如果你是产品经理，标准可以换成：

它会不会影响用户路径？

能不能降低客服、销售、运营或者内容生产成本？

有没有竞品已经开始验证？

能不能变成一个具体的产品功能点？

这里有个关键点：

AI 不是替你拥有判断力。

AI 是把你的判断标准，稳定、重复地执行下去。

### 01:45 - 02:45 第二步：把信息源丢进同一个入口

第二步，把你的信息源集中到一个入口。

不用一开始搞得很复杂。

最简单的版本，只需要三类信息。

第一类，是你每天会看的公开信息。

比如 newsletter、X、LinkedIn、Product Hunt、GitHub Trending、行业网站、YouTube 摘要、播客文字稿。

第二类，是你自己的工作上下文。

你现在在做什么产品，负责什么增长目标，正在研究什么方向，最近遇到什么问题。

第三类，是你的能力和资源边界。

你会不会写代码，有没有用户渠道，每周能投入多少时间，有没有设计、销售、运营能力。

然后你给 AI 一个非常明确的任务：

不要给我十条新闻。

不要给我长篇趋势分析。

每天只输出三条值得行动的信息。

每条信息必须包含五个字段：

第一，原始信息是什么。

第二，为什么和我有关。

第三，它背后的机会或风险是什么。

第四，我今天可以做的最小动作是什么。

第五，如果不做，能不能直接丢弃。

最后一个字段很重要。

一个好的信息过滤器，不只是帮你发现机会。

它更重要的价值，是帮你减少噪音。

### 02:45 - 03:45 具体例子：同一条信息，普通总结和行动信号的差别

举个例子。

假设今天你看到一个开源项目，可以把网页、PDF、文档自动转成结构化 Markdown。

普通 AI 总结大概会这么说：

“这是一个用于网页解析和内容提取的开源工具，适合研究和知识管理。”

听起来没错，但没有什么用。

因为你听完以后，还是不知道今天该干什么。

一个合格的信息过滤器，应该这样输出：

原始信息：某个开源项目支持把网页、PDF 和文档转换成干净的 Markdown，并保留结构。

为什么和你有关：你正在做 AI 自动化周报，需要稳定处理大量网页信息，这个工具可能降低内容采集和整理成本。

机会：可以把它接进你的信息收集流程，先把文章变成统一格式，再交给 AI 做筛选、评分和摘要。

今天最小动作：拿三篇不同来源的文章测试解析质量，记录失败场景。如果中文网页和 PDF 都过关，再放进下周 workflow。

是否丢弃：如果解析质量不稳定，先丢弃，不进入主流程。

你看，这就不是“我又知道了一个工具”。

而是“我知道今天要测试什么”。

这就是信息消费和工作流之间的差别。

### 03:45 - 04:25 第三步：每周复盘，让系统变聪明

第三步，每周复盘一次。

如果每天筛出三条行动信号，一周就是十五到二十一条。

周末你只问三个问题。

第一，哪些信号真的值得做？

第二，哪些看起来很热，但其实和我没关系？

第三，AI 哪些判断错了，我的过滤标准要怎么改？

这个动作做四周，你会发现一件事：

你不是在做一个信息摘要工具。

你是在训练自己的个人机会雷达。

它会越来越清楚你关注什么，忽略什么，适合做什么，不适合追什么。

很多人以为 AI 自动化的价值，是让机器替你干活。

但更深一层，是让机器替你持续执行一套判断流程。

这样你的注意力，才不会每天被信息流牵着走。

### 04:25 - 05:10 结尾：悬念和关注引导

接下来几期，我会继续拆这种可以直接复制的 AI workflow。

比如：

怎么用 AI 做一个竞品监控系统，每周自动告诉你对手改了什么；

怎么把客服、销售、用户反馈，整理成产品需求池；

怎么把一条行业新闻，自动变成选题、邮件、销售跟进和团队任务；

以及，怎么把一个内部提效流程，包装成一个可以对外验证的小产品。

这里面真正有价值的，不是某一个工具名。

工具会变，模型会变，平台也会变。

不变的是：

你能不能把一个模糊的问题，拆成一个可执行的流程；

再把这个流程，变成每周稳定产出的结果。

这就是 AI Operator Brief 想做的事。

每周不追热点，只拆一个能落地的 AI workflow、工具链、提示词和真实案例。

如果你也是独立开发者、产品经理、运营增长，或者正在用 AI 搭自己的工作系统，欢迎关注。

下一期，我会把这个“每日信息过滤器”的完整 prompt 结构拆出来。

包括怎么打分，怎么过滤垃圾信息，怎么避免 AI 给你一堆看起来正确但完全没法行动的废话。

很多人和信息流的关系，是被推着走。

真正的 operator，要学会反过来筛选信息。

这可能就是普通 AI 使用者和 AI operator 之间的第一道分水岭。

## English Voiceover Script

Suggested title:
You’re Not Bad at AI. You Just Haven’t Plugged It Into Your Workflow.

### 00:00 - 00:35 Opening: professional anxiety

A lot of people think they’re already using AI.

They open ChatGPT, ask a few questions, rewrite a paragraph, summarize an article, generate a few headlines.

And yes, that is useful.

But here’s the uncomfortable part:

If that’s the only way you use AI, it probably won’t create much of an advantage.

The real gap is not between people who know prompts and people who don’t.

The real gap is between people who use AI as a chat box, and people who plug AI into the way they actually work every day.

Because the problem now is not lack of information.

It’s the opposite.

Too many tools. Too many launches. Too many takes. Too many “opportunities.”

You can spend the whole day reading, saving, forwarding, bookmarking — and still end the day without moving one important thing forward.

That’s the dangerous part.

You didn’t miss the information.

You lost the ability to turn information into action.

### 00:35 - 01:05 The problem: stop using AI only as a chat box

So today I’m not going to talk about another new AI tool.

And I’m not going to make some grand claim about how AI changes everything.

I want to show you one simple workflow that every knowledge worker should build first:

a daily AI signal filter.

Think of it as a research assistant that works only for you.

Its job is not to help you consume more news.

Its job is to look at messy inputs and give you three signals worth acting on today.

Not summaries.

Signals.

A summary tells you what happened.

A signal tells you why it matters to you, what you can do next, and whether you should ignore it completely.

That difference matters.

### 01:05 - 01:45 Step one: define your filter

The first step is not opening Notion.

It’s not connecting an API.

The first step is defining your filter.

Most people get poor results from AI research because they start with a weak instruction:

“Summarize these ten articles.”

That gives you content.

But what you need is judgment.

If you’re a solo builder, your filter might look like this:

Can this become a small demo within seven days?

Is there a clear user pain behind it?

Can it connect to tools I already use — Notion, Slack, Telegram, Stripe, Airtable, or email?

Is there a possible paid use case?

Can one person or a very small team actually execute it?

If you’re a product person, the filter changes:

Does this affect the user journey?

Can it reduce support, sales, operations, or content workload?

Are competitors already testing it?

Could it become a real feature, not just an interesting idea?

This is the key point:

AI does not magically give you judgment.

AI scales the judgment criteria you give it.

### 01:45 - 02:45 Step two: send your inputs into one place

The second step is to route your information sources into one place.

You don’t need to overbuild this at the beginning.

Start with three types of inputs.

First, public information you already read:

newsletters, X, LinkedIn, Product Hunt, GitHub Trending, industry blogs, YouTube transcripts, podcast summaries.

Second, your own work context:

what product you’re building, what growth goal you care about, what market you’re researching, what problems you’re currently trying to solve.

Third, your constraints:

Can you code?

Do you have an audience?

How much time can you spend each week?

Do you have design, sales, or operations support?

Then give the AI a very specific job:

Don’t give me ten news items.

Don’t give me a long trend report.

Give me only three pieces of information worth acting on today.

Each one must include five fields:

What is the original information?

Why is it relevant to me?

What opportunity or risk does it suggest?

What is the smallest action I can take today?

And if I don’t act on it, can I safely ignore it?

That last field is important.

A good signal filter does not only find opportunities.

It removes noise.

### 02:45 - 03:45 Example: summary versus signal

Here’s a simple example.

Imagine you find an open-source tool that turns web pages, PDFs, and documents into clean structured Markdown.

A normal AI summary might say:

“This is an open-source tool for web parsing and content extraction, useful for research and knowledge management.”

That sounds fine.

But it doesn’t help you decide what to do.

A useful signal filter would say something like this:

Original information:
An open-source project can convert web pages, PDFs, and documents into clean Markdown while preserving structure.

Why it matters:
You are building an AI workflow brief and need a reliable way to process large amounts of online content. This tool may reduce the cost of collecting and cleaning research material.

Opportunity:
It could become the first step in your research pipeline — normalize content first, then let AI score, filter, and summarize it.

Smallest action today:
Test it on three articles from different sources. Record where it fails. If it handles messy web pages and PDFs well, add it to next week’s workflow.

Ignore or keep:
If the output is unstable, ignore it for now. Don’t let it enter the main system.

See the difference?

This is not “I found another tool.”

This is “I know exactly what to test today.”

That is the difference between consuming information and operating with a workflow.

### 03:45 - 04:25 Step three: review weekly and train your judgment

The third step is a weekly review.

If your filter gives you three signals a day, that’s fifteen to twenty-one signals per week.

At the end of the week, ask three questions:

Which signals were actually worth acting on?

Which ones looked hot, but had nothing to do with my work?

Where did the AI make the wrong call, and how should I change the filter?

After four weeks, something interesting happens.

You’re no longer just building a summarization tool.

You’re building a personal signal radar.

It starts to reflect what you care about, what you ignore, what fits your constraints, and what you should not chase.

A lot of people think AI automation means letting machines do the work.

But the deeper value is this:

AI can repeatedly run a judgment process that you design.

That protects your attention from being dragged around by the feed every day.

### 04:25 - 05:10 Closing: suspense and CTA

In the next few issues, I’ll break down more workflows like this.

For example:

how to build a competitor monitoring system that tells you what changed every week;

how to turn support messages, sales calls, and user feedback into a product opportunity list;

how to turn one industry news item into content ideas, outbound messages, and team tasks;

and how to take an internal productivity workflow and test whether it could become a small external product.

The valuable part is not the name of a tool.

Tools change. Models change. Platforms change.

What does not change is the ability to take a vague problem, break it into a repeatable process, and turn that process into consistent output.

That is what AI Operator Brief is about.

No hype. No endless news feed.

Just one practical AI workflow, toolchain, prompt structure, or real operating case at a time.

If you’re a solo builder, product person, operator, growth person, or someone building your own AI-powered work system, follow along.

Next time, I’ll share the exact prompt structure for this daily signal filter — including the scoring rules I use to stop AI from giving generic summaries that sound smart but lead nowhere.

Most people are pushed around by the feed.

Operators learn to filter it.

And that may be the first real line between someone who uses AI casually and someone who operates with it.
